The mood-squashing robot is not some sort of mechanized Mrs. Grundy. It is a bot -- an artificial intelligence program designed to perform simple chores. This one is specifically a clonebot, rogue software purposely invented to wreak havoc by self-replicating and rudely interrupting live interaction in Internet chat areas.

In his ingenious ''Bots: The Origin of New Species,'' Leonard, a writer on technology, takes an in-depth look at the various little automated bits of indigenous computer code roaming the bandwidth of cyberspace, and at how these aptly named mailbots, chatterbots, spambots, searchbots and their kind affect the lives of Internet users every day.

The clonebot vignette brilliantly captures the basic pro and con of bot reality: it's cool to have a digital drone to do one's bidding, but it's hell when the thing gets loose and runs amok. ''As the interface between human and computer, as anthropomorphic reflections of our own deepest character traits, as plowshares that can be hammered into swords, bots will exemplify all our deepest hopes and fears,'' Leonard writes.

Some bots are more complex than others, and some definitely were designed for tasks higher than playing post office. ''If bots are hot,'' Leonard says, ''then chatterbots are one of the main reasons why: they capture the imagination in a way that a staid mailbot, with its formulaic messages about incorrectly addressed mail or network inadequacies, never will.'' The chatterbot, a neatly packaged collection of algorithms designed to mimic and carry on a conversation, first appeared in 1966 and has been programmed to act more humanlike since, sometimes passing for a real person in the physically formless void of the Net.

With ''Bots,'' Leonard has also constructed a playful social history of the Internet, tracking it from the free-form ASCII-text playground of chipheads, researchers and scientists to the sprawling, advertising-laden cultural entity it has become. Recognizing the World Wide Web as a turning point for both Internet growth and the intentions of the bots themselves, Leonard details the missions of searchbots, spambots and shopping bots that do everything from flooding E-mailboxes with ''make money fast'' junk mail to creating ''user profiles'' of the likes, dislikes and favorite electronic hangouts of Web surfers on behalf of advertisers.

Leonard's ruminations on the sinister side of botdom are particularly interesting, mainly because they play into the paranoia and fears many responsible Netizens have already expressed over matters of on-line confidentiality. Far more serious than an obnoxious clonebot disrupting virtual seduction, it is the issue of a very real invasion of privacy that Leonard brings to the reader's attention.

Writing in a blend of English and cyberjargon, Leonard cleverly uses ''Bots'' as a vehicle to explore the psychology and anthropology of popular technology. His relaxed yet knowledgeable tone occasionally borders on a note of cynical whimsy about the whole artificial intelligence phenomenon. ''Any culture that can go mad over pet rocks,'' he dryly observes, ''can certainly fall in love with a computer program.''

J. D. Biersdorfer is a member of the newsroom technology staff at The New York Times.
